Legislative Effectiveness

  • We can think about legislative effectiveness in two ways:

    • Institutional Effectiveness

      • Congresss governing responsibilities

    • Individual Effectiveness

      • Participating in the lawmaking process and representing their constituents

Institutional Effectiveness

  • We can think about this in terms of performance over time

    • Lawmaking, oversight, constituent service

  • We shall focus on lawmaking

    • In order to keep the government operational Congress must perform the following tasks:

      • Authorizations

      • Annual budgets and appropriations

      • Federal debt

      • Major legislation

    • How well do we think Congress meets its responsibilities in these categories?

Authorizations

  • Authorizations

    • Establish or renew federal programs, agencies, and policies

  • Authorizations can be either short term or long term

    • Short term: Few months or years

    • Long term: permanent or multiple years

  • Short term authorizations go back to the Framers

    • Short term authorizations are more common

    • Works a safe guard

      • Groups can sound the alarm on bad policy

    • Provides committees with agenda control

    • Keeps interest groups engaged

      • Keeps donations flowing

  • What happens when authorizations do not occur?

    • In many instances, agencies can continue on with day to day operations

    • Congress can issue the agency a waiver

      • Which provides the needed funds without reauthorization

The Budget and Appropriations Process

  • The budget process

    • Authorizing legislation

    • Budget resolution

    • appropriations legislation

  • The president generally starts the budget process every February

    • Proposes a budget for the entire federal government

      • Includes estimates for revenues and expenditures, projections for future years, and budget requests for each agency

  • Congress budget committees will hold hearings on the proposed budget

  • By mid-April, the House and Senate are required to produce their concurrent budget resolution on the budget

    • Provide a target for spending, which guides congress when they are completing the appropriations bill

  • This is conducted annually

Appropriations

  • Appropriations bills allow the Treasury to make payments for specific programs or agencies

  • The Constitutions outlines the appropriations process

    • Article I, Section 9: No money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by law

  • Appropriations bills deal with discretionary spending

    • Money for things that the Executive and Legislative branches can change

  • Appropriation bills do not change mandatory spending

    • Spending that happens automatically every year and does not need a appropriations bill

      • Interest on national debt, contractual obligations, entitlement spending

  • Entitlement programs

    • Large government spending programs

      • Social Security, Medicare, Veterans Compensation

  • Congress routinely fails to pass all appropriations measures separately

    • Lumps them together in omnibus bills

  • Additionally, with increasing polarization, Congress rarely passes appropriations bills on time

    • Continuing Resolutions (CR)

      • Measure to fund programs and agencies until regular appropriations can be finalized

      • These are on the rise

Funding Gaps and the Federal Debt Limit

  • Government shutdowns occur when a continuing resolution cannot be passes (or better yet regular appropriations bills)

  • Based on spending, we run at a deficit

    • We spend more money than we take in annually with tax revenue

  • What happens if we default?
