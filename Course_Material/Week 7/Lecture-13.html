Lecture 13 | Policy Making in the House and Senate

Plan for Today

  • Discuss the floor process in the Senate

Senate Process

  • The Senate is considered an individual body

  • Very autonomous body that has the ability to deliberate and compromise

  • But if the Senate constantly had to give way to each member, then nothing would get accomplished

UCAs in the Senate

  • Unanimous Consent Agreements

    • Allow the Senate to avoid chamber rules that would bog down the process

    • Structure debate, amendments, and timing of the vote

      • Similar to special rules in the House

      • Difference is that every senator must agree to it

    • Negotiated between the majority and minority party leadership (and some other members like committee members or partisan leaders)

Germaneness in the Senate

  • The Senate has no rules relating to the germaneness of a bill/topic

  • Senators can offer practically any amendment to a bill being considered on the floor

    • Assuming it adheres to the UCA

Withholding Consent

  • In recent years, we have seen an increase in members not agreeing to UCAs, thereby threatening to extend debate on a bill

  • Filibuster

    • Is any tactic that blocks a measure on the Senate floor from coming to a vote

      • Generally, in the form of a single lawmaker refusing to end debate by making a long uninterrupted speech

  • How are filibusters possible?

    • Chamber rules require the presiding officer to recognize all senators wishing to speak

    • The Senate lacks any formal provision regarding time limits on debate

  • How is a filibuster conducted

    • Once recognized, the senator may speak at length, without yielding the floor, but must remain standing and speaking

    • Senate procedure prohibits members from yielding the floor to other members

      • Questions from other senators is not prohibited

        • Therefore, a colleague can help a filibustering member by asking a very long question

  • Longest individual filibuster: Strom Thurmond (D-SC) 24 hours and 18 minutes

    • Filibuster the Civil Rights Act of 1957

    • Started speaking at 8:54pm on August 28 and continued until 9:12pm the following day

      • Recited the Declaration of Independence, Bill of Rights, Washington’s Farewell Address and other historical documents

      • Failed to prevent passage of the bill

  • Holds

    • A more informal version of a filibuster

      • Informal in that it is not specified in the chamber rules

    • A hold registers a senator’s intention to object to any UCA

    • Called a silent filibuster

    • Usually for less salient legislation

Cloture

  • How do we stop/avoid a filibuster?

  • Invoke Cloture

    • Limits debate to 30 hours

    • 3/5s of the members are required (60)

  • Established in 1971

    • Originally, needed 2/3s of members (67)

  • Generally, required members from both parties

    • Exception was in 1975 and 1978 (Democrats)

Amendments in the Senate

  • Once a bill reaches the Senate floor it is open to amendments at any time

    • Assuming it is within the UCA

  • Types of amendments in the Senate

    • First-Degree Amendment

      • Changing the text of the bill

    • Second-Degree Amendment

      • Changing the text of an amendment

    • Striking language from the bill

    • Inserting new language into a bill

    • Replacing the entire bill with new language

  • The amendment tree

  • The amendment tree, by nature of being recognized first, is controlled by the majority party leader

    • Therefore, the majority party leader can effectively control what (and who) is introduced as amendments

Final Thoughts

  • The legislative process is a long and arduous task
